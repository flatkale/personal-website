December 2025

Reflections from an acquisition

The day we signed, I went for a walk and felt nothing I could name. Not euphoria. Not relief. Something closer to what you feel at the end of a long book, when the thing you'd been living inside suddenly isn't there anymore.

Dan Gilbert at Harvard spent years studying what he called the "end of history illusion." People consistently believe that who they are today is basically who they'll always be. We know we've changed a lot in the past. We assume we won't change much in the future. He found this was wrong, universally, across cultures and ages. I mention this because I had the same illusion about the acquisition. I was very confident I knew what I'd feel. I was completely wrong.

The grief surprised me most. Not grief as in sadness exactly. More like the grief of a chapter ending. For five years, the outcome of Secoda was genuinely unknown. It could have been nothing. That uncertainty was uncomfortable but it was also, I realized afterward, the fuel. The not-yet-known quality of the thing was part of what made it worth waking up for. Once it resolved, that particular source of energy went away.

Peter Thiel has a line I keep coming back to: "every moment in business happens only once." The next Gates won't build an operating system, the next Zuckerberg won't build a social network. Thiel means this as a prompt for original thinking. But I think about it differently now. Each version of your company exists only once too. The Secoda that was figuring out product-market fit, that was running out of runway, that was signing its first enterprise customer -- those versions existed and now they don't. That's worth sitting with.

What I got wrong: I underestimated how much of the company's identity I'd borrowed from the uncertainty itself. Strip away the uncertainty and you have to build a new relationship with the work. That takes longer than the legal process.

What I got right: the team. I don't mean I hired perfectly, I didn't. I mean that I learned, slowly, that the quality of the people around you is the only thing that compounds in a way that matters. Revenue can go to zero. Reputation can be rebuilt. A team that genuinely trusts each other and wants to solve hard problems together -- that's the real asset, and it's the one that's hardest to rebuild once it's gone.

The thing nobody tells you is that selling a company doesn't end the work. It changes the constraints. If you're lucky, it changes them in ways that let you do things you couldn't before. I'm still figuring out which new things those are.
