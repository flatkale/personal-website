Canada doesn't have a talent problem

The conventional story about Canadian tech is that the best people leave. They move to San Francisco, build companies there, and Canada watches its human capital drain south. The policy response is always some version of the same thing: better immigration, better incentives, better salaries. Keep the talent here.

I think this is solving the wrong problem.

G.K. Chesterton had a principle that Paul Graham quotes often: don't tear down a fence until you understand why it was built. I want to apply it here. Why did Canadian modesty become a cultural norm in the first place? Because in most contexts, it works. Canada is a high-trust, low-conflict society. Those are genuinely valuable properties. Modesty is social lubricant in a culture that runs on social cohesion.

The problem is that building a startup requires you to be impolite to reality in specific ways. You have to tell people your unproven idea is going to be huge. You have to recruit someone from a stable job into your chaos. You have to charge more than seems reasonable for something that doesn't fully exist yet. Modesty is actively harmful here. Not marginally harmful. Structurally harmful.

The people who leave for San Francisco aren't leaving because of money, mostly. They're leaving because in San Francisco, saying you're building something important is treated as a normal thing to say. In Toronto, the same sentence gets a raised eyebrow. That difference in social environment changes what feels possible. Tocqueville noticed something like this about democracy in America: the majority's opinion doesn't just constrain behavior, it constrains imagination. The Canadian majority's opinion about what constitutes a reasonable ambition quietly caps what founders think they're allowed to want.

The talent is here. Waterloo produces extraordinary engineers. Toronto attracts exceptional immigrants. The University of Toronto's AI research program is world-class. Yoshua Bengio at MILA in Montreal helped invent the technology that every major AI company is now built on. The raw material is not the constraint.

What's missing is a critical mass of people who've done it and stayed. Tobi Lutke built Shopify in Ottawa and didn't move. That matters more than any government grant program, because the most powerful thing an ambitious young Canadian can encounter is an ambitious older Canadian who didn't leave and still won. Role models are infrastructure. We don't have enough of them yet.

When I was building Secoda, I made the mistake early on of benchmarking against Canadian outcomes. What was a good Canadian B2B SaaS exit? What was a strong Canadian Series A? Those are the wrong questions. They're not the questions my American competitors were asking. The moment you start measuring yourself against a local bracket, you've accepted a ceiling nobody forced on you.

Build here. The time zones are fine. The engineers are great. The cost basis is genuinely advantageous. But think without the geographic qualifier. That part is entirely optional.
