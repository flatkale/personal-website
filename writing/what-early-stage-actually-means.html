What early stage actually means

The economist Frank Knight made a distinction in 1921 that most people still haven't absorbed. Risk, he said, is uncertainty you can put a number on. You can price risk. Insurance exists because of it. Uncertainty is different: it's when you don't even know the distribution of outcomes. You can't price it. You just have to act anyway.

Early stage is pure Knight uncertainty. Not risk. Uncertainty. The founder who treats these as the same thing will optimize for the wrong things from the start.

The mistake this produces is hiring risk frameworks -- spreadsheets, OKRs, forecasts -- onto a situation where the inputs are invented. I've watched smart teams spend weeks debating assumptions in a model when the right move was to spend those weeks finding out whether the assumptions were real. The model is not the map. The customer is the map.

John Boyd was a fighter pilot who developed a theory of decision-making called the OODA loop: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. His insight, which took military strategy decades to absorb, was that speed through the loop beats accuracy within the loop. A pilot who makes three imperfect decisions while the enemy makes one careful decision wins. Not because any of his decisions were better. Because he's generated more information faster.

This is what early stage actually is. Not a phase of building. A phase of information generation. The question isn't "are we building the right thing?" It's "how quickly can we find out whether we're building the right thing?"

When I started Secoda, we had a theory about who the customer was and what they needed. The theory was wrong in a specific way: we thought the pain was in finding data. The actual pain was in trusting data. Those seem similar. They're not. They lead to completely different products. We found this out by doing customer calls where we mostly listened, which we didn't do enough of early on because building felt more productive than listening. It isn't.

The other thing early stage means is that the people around you are more important than any other variable. Not their skills, exactly. Their character. Specifically: do they tell you the truth? Bad news travels fast inside some companies and slowly inside others, and this property is set in the first few months. You set it by rewarding people who bring problems early, and visibly not rewarding people who hide them.

I think founders underestimate how much their behavior in the first year is being studied. The team is running experiments on what you actually care about, not what you say you care about. Whatever you respond to becomes the culture. You don't get a later chance to revise the methodology.

Ship something. Talk to the person who used it. Find out what was wrong. Do it again. The sophistication of your planning is essentially uncorrelated with outcomes at this stage. The quality of your feedback loops is the whole game.
